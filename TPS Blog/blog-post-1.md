# What is ISPM-15? A Guide to Heat-Treated Pallets for Export

If you've ever had a shipment held at customs because of "non-compliant wood packaging," you've already met ISPM-15 the hard way. For everyone else, this is the regulation worth understanding before your first international shipment, not after.

Here's what it is, why it exists, and what to ask when you order pallets for export.

## The short version

ISPM-15 is an international standard that requires solid wood packaging materials — pallets, crates, dunnage — to be treated to kill any pests living inside the wood before they cross borders. Without compliant packaging, your shipment can be rejected, fumigated at your expense, or sent back to the country of origin.

The standard is enforced in over 180 countries, including the United States, every European Union nation, China, Australia, and most major trading partners. If you're shipping anywhere outside Canada, ISPM-15 likely applies.

## Why the regulation exists

The short answer: bugs. The longer answer is that wooden pallets and crates have historically been one of the main vectors for invasive insects spreading between continents. The Asian longhorned beetle, the emerald ash borer, the pinewood nematode — all of these have caused billions of dollars in forest damage globally, and untreated wood packaging played a role in spreading them.

ISPM-15 was introduced by the International Plant Protection Convention in 2002 to standardize how wood packaging is treated worldwide. Before that, every country had its own rules and shippers had to decode dozens of different requirements.

## How pallets become ISPM-15 compliant

There are two approved treatment methods.

### Heat treatment (HT)

The pallet is heated to a core temperature of at least 56°C for a minimum of 30 minutes. This kills any insects, larvae, or eggs in the wood without using chemicals. Heat treatment is the most common method in North America because it's clean, repeatable, and doesn't affect how the pallet performs.

### Methyl bromide fumigation (MB)

The pallet is exposed to methyl bromide gas in a sealed chamber. This method is being phased out in most countries because methyl bromide is an ozone-depleting chemical. Heat treatment is now the standard in Canada and most of our trading partners.

If you're sourcing pallets in Canada today, they should be heat-treated, not fumigated.

## What the certification stamp looks like

A compliant ISPM-15 pallet has a permanent mark — usually branded or stenciled — that includes:

- The IPPC logo (a wheat-stalk symbol inside an ear-of-wheat icon)
- A two-letter country code (CA for Canada)
- A unique registration number identifying the producer
- The treatment code (HT for heat-treated, MB for fumigated)

The mark must appear on at least two opposite sides of the pallet, and it must be legible. Faded, smudged, or missing marks are grounds for rejection at the border.

## Common reasons shipments get rejected

Even with the right intent, mistakes happen. The most common reasons we see for ISPM-15 issues:

- **Mismatched mark and treatment.** A pallet stamped HT that wasn't actually heat-treated to spec. This is why sourcing from a certified producer matters more than just looking at the stamp.
- **Damaged or covered marks.** A label slapped over the IPPC mark, or wear-and-tear that makes the registration number unreadable.
- **Mixing compliant and non-compliant wood.** A pallet built mostly with treated wood but using one untreated stringer or block.
- **Reused packaging from a previous shipment.** Pallets from a foreign shipment can't necessarily be reused for another international trip without re-inspection.

## What to ask your pallet supplier

Before you place an order for export pallets, three questions are worth asking:

**1. Are you a certified ISPM-15 producer in Canada?**
The supplier should have a CFIA-issued registration number. Without one, they can't legally apply the IPPC mark, even if their pallets are technically heat-treated.

**2. What's your treatment process and how do you verify temperature?**
Reputable suppliers heat-treat in-house with logged temperature monitoring. Outsourced or unverified processes are a red flag.

**3. How do you handle the IPPC stamp placement and durability?**
The stamp needs to survive shipping. Permanent branding holds up better than ink stencils, especially on pallets exposed to moisture.
