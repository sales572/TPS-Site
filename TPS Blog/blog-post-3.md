# 5 Questions to Ask Before Choosing a Pallet Supplier

If you're buying pallets for the first time, or reconsidering an existing supplier, the temptation is to compare quotes line by line and pick the cheapest one. We get it — pallets are usually a cost line, not a revenue line, and saving a dollar per pallet adds up fast at volume.

But pallets are also operationally critical. A late delivery shuts down your shipping bay. A weak pallet breaks under a forklift and damages your product. A non-compliant pallet sends an export shipment back from customs.

Here are five questions that surface the difference between a supplier who'll save you money long-term and one who'll cost you on every order after the first.

## 1. How quickly do you respond to quote requests?

Sounds basic, but it's the most reliable predictor of how the relationship will go. A supplier who takes three days to send a quote will take three days to return your call when there's a delivery problem.

Good suppliers respond to quotes within one business day, often within hours. Great ones answer the phone the first time you call.

The reason this matters: pallet ordering is rarely planned far in advance. Most calls go out when an operations team realizes they're running low or has a sudden order coming in. If your supplier isn't responsive at the quote stage, they won't be responsive when you need a rush delivery either.

## 2. What's your delivery network and lead time?

The cheapest pallet quote is meaningless if you can't get the pallets when you need them. Ask:

- Where is the production facility located? (Closer is faster and usually cheaper to deliver.)
- Do you deliver on your own trucks, or contract it out?
- What's typical lead time for a standard order? For a custom order?
- What happens if I need a rush order?

Suppliers with their own delivery fleet have control over their delivery schedule. Suppliers who outsource delivery are at the mercy of their freight partners — which is fine until something goes wrong.

For GTA businesses, a Toronto-area supplier with their own trucks can usually deliver next-day on standard orders. Suppliers shipping from outside Ontario almost always quote 3–5 business days, and weather or freight issues stretch that further.

## 3. Are you certified for the products you sell?

If you're shipping internationally — or might in the future — your supplier needs to be ISPM-15 certified to produce export-compliant pallets. The relevant certifications in Canada:

- **CFIA registration** for ISPM-15 heat treatment (Canadian Food Inspection Agency)
- **IPPC** authorization to apply the international heat-treatment mark
- **NWPCA** membership (National Wooden Pallet & Container Association — North American industry standard)
- **CWPCA** membership (Canadian Wood Pallet & Container Association)

Ask for certification numbers, not just "yes we're certified." A real registration number is verifiable; a vague claim isn't.

Even if you're only shipping domestically today, sourcing from a certified supplier protects your future flexibility. Switching suppliers is annoying; switching to one with credentials you didn't know you'd need is worse.

## 4. How do you handle quality control?

This is where the cheap-quote suppliers get caught. The lumber, hardware, and assembly quality of pallets varies more than buyers realize. A pallet that looks the same as another can have:

- Soft, unseasoned wood that splits under load
- Hardware that's slightly thinner than spec and pulls out under stress
- Inconsistent stringer placement that causes uneven weight distribution
- Mixed wood species in a single pallet, with different strength ratings

Ask the supplier:

- Is your lumber seasoned to a target moisture content?
- Do you grade your pallets before shipping (Grade A, B, etc.)?
- What happens if I receive a pallet that fails in normal use?

Good suppliers have a quality grading process and stand behind it. If a pallet fails, they replace it without an argument.

## 5. What's your approach to recycling and sustainability?

Two reasons this question matters. The obvious one: more buyers — and especially more enterprise customers — are tracking supply chain sustainability metrics. If you supply a major retailer or distributor, they may require you to report on packaging environmental impact.

The less obvious reason: a supplier who runs a real recycling program has a more efficient operation. They reuse usable boards, repair pallets that have life left in them, and waste less wood. That efficiency tends to translate into more consistent pricing because they're less exposed to lumber price swings.

Ask:

- Do you accept used pallets back for recycling?
- What percentage of your lumber is recycled or repaired?
- Do you offer pallet pickup as a service?

A "yes" to all three suggests a supplier who's invested in the long-term operation. A "no" to all three is fine if you don't care about those things — but it does tell you something about how they think about their business.

## What this looks like in practice

You don't need to interview five suppliers in depth. Send a quote request to two or three and listen for how quickly and clearly they answer. The questions above will surface naturally in the conversation if the supplier knows what they're doing — and the ones who can't answer them confidently will tell you something just by the way they hesitate.
