# Standard 48×40 vs Custom Pallets: How to Choose

When operations managers call us for the first time, one of the most common questions is also one of the most overlooked: "Do we actually need custom pallets, or can we use standard?" The honest answer is that for most businesses, standard works. For some, custom saves money. Knowing which camp you're in is worth thinking through before you place an order.

Here's a practical guide to making the decision.

## Why 48×40 became the standard

The 48-inch by 40-inch pallet — sometimes called the GMA pallet, after the Grocery Manufacturers Association that defined it — is the most widely used pallet size in North America. About 30% of all pallets in circulation in the U.S. and Canada are this size.

There's nothing magical about the dimensions. The size emerged because it fits efficiently in standard 53-foot trailers (you can fit 30 pallets per trailer in a two-row layout), works with standard warehouse racking systems, and matches the forklift and pallet-jack tooling that nearly every distribution center has.

In other words: it's standard because the entire supply chain is built around it.

## When the standard 48×40 is the right choice

You should default to the 48×40 pallet if any of the following describe your operation:

**Your product is being shipped through major retailers or distributors.** Walmart, Costco, Loblaws, Home Depot, and most other major buyers expect 48×40 pallets. Sending custom-sized pallets means your goods get re-palletized at the receiving warehouse, which costs you money and creates damage risk.

**You ship through third-party logistics or LTL carriers.** Carrier pricing is built around standard pallet footprints. Non-standard sizes often trigger oversize fees or get re-handled.

**Your product fits on the pallet without significant overhang or wasted space.** If a 48×40 holds your goods well, custom is solving a problem you don't have.

**You want pallet exchange programs to work.** Many large shippers run "pallet pool" programs where compliant pallets are returned and reused. These programs only accept standard sizes.

## When custom pallets make financial sense

Custom isn't about preference — it's about whether your product fits the standard or fights it. Custom pallets are worth the upfront cost when:

**Your product is significantly smaller or larger than 48×40.** If your goods leave a foot of empty space on every pallet, you're paying to ship air. A custom-sized pallet that matches your product means more units per truckload, lower freight costs, and less wasted space.

**You're shipping high-value or fragile goods.** Custom pallets can be designed with specific deck-board spacing, reinforced stringers, or block construction that protects your specific product. Generic pallets can't.

**You need export-ready pallets at unusual sizes.** Some international destinations have their own standards. Europe uses 1200×800mm (the EUR pallet); Asia often uses 1100×1100mm. Sending North American 48×40 pallets to those markets sometimes works but isn't optimal.

**You have specific weight or load requirements.** A custom-engineered pallet using PDS (Pallet Design System) software can be built to exactly the weight capacity your product needs — no more, no less. Over-built pallets waste money on lumber. Under-built ones fail in transit.

**Your warehouse racking is non-standard.** Older facilities, retrofitted spaces, or specialized industries sometimes have racks that 48×40 doesn't fit cleanly. Custom pallets sized for your racks improve storage density.

## A simple decision framework

If you're not sure which way to lean, ask yourself three questions:

1. **Does my product fit on a 48×40 pallet without major overhang or wasted space?** If yes, lean standard.
2. **Do my customers or distributors require a specific pallet size?** If yes, that decides it.
3. **Am I shipping enough volume that small efficiency gains compound into real money?** If yes, the math may justify custom.

Two yeses for "standard" usually means standard is the right call. Two yeses for "custom" makes the case for engineering.

## What custom actually costs

Custom pallets cost more per unit than standard 48×40 pallets — typically 15% to 40% more, depending on the size and design complexity. The premium reflects three things: lumber sized to a non-standard cut, a one-time setup for the production run, and the engineering time to design the pallet correctly.

The trade-off is worth it when you're saving more on freight, damage, or efficiency than you're paying in pallet premium. For some operations that's true at high volume; for others, it's never true.

## How to get the right call

The fastest way to figure out whether custom makes sense is to share your product dimensions, weight, and shipping destinations with a pallet supplier and let them run the numbers. A good supplier won't push you toward custom if standard will do — there's no upside for them, and over-spec pallets are a waste for everyone.
